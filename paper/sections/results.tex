\subsection{Primary Metrics}

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} reports overall REFUTE performance.
E1 (7B) achieves perfect precision with 21 refutations out of 92 conjectures.
Recall of $0.292$ means 51 of 72 truly-false conjectures are not refuted
by the 7B model on this benchmark.
E2 (32B int4) produces identical recall to E1: precision $1.000$, recall
$0.292$, F$_1$ $0.452$, all 21 refutations via BOUNDARY.

\subsection{Tier and Domain Breakdown}

Table~\ref{tab:breakdown} disaggregates results by tier and domain.
Several patterns are immediately apparent:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Number theory dominates.}
    All 21 E1 refutations are in number theory (recall $0.389$).
    Inequality and combinatorics recall is $0.000$ under both runs.

  \item \textbf{Tier 1 ceiling is low.}
    Even on synthetic-false conjectures (Tier~1), recall is $0.339$,
    indicating that 39 of 59 straightforwardly false conjectures escape
    refutation.

  \item \textbf{Tier 2 / Tier 3 recall is near zero.}
    Only 1 of 6 false Tier~2 conjectures is refuted (the
    $n^2+1$ primality conjecture, refuted by $n=4$).
    Tier~3 recall is $0.000$, consistent with its design as a specificity test.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Strategy Attribution}

Every E1 refutation was produced by the \textbf{BOUNDARY} strategy.
RANDOM\_STRUCTURED, SYMBOLIC\_PERTURBATION, and ANALOGICAL contributed zero
wins despite being exercised on all 71 survived conjectures (post-patch,
each received at least BOUNDARY + RANDOM\_STRUCTURED).
This finding motivates the 32B experiment: SYMBOLIC\_PERTURBATION and
ANALOGICAL are LLM-dependent and may require stronger generation capability
to produce verifiable candidates.
E2 (32B) confirms this pattern: all 21 refutations remain attributed to
BOUNDARY. Five survived conjectures received all four strategies (vs.\ at most
two in E1), yet none yielded a verified counterexample, indicating that
increased strategy coverage alone did not suffice to improve recall within
this experimental setting.
